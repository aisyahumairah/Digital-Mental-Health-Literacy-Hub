Common Warning Signs

Academic Warning Signs

  • Sudden decline in academic performance
  • Repeated absences or tardiness
  • Missing assignments or exams
  • Difficulty concentrating or focusing
  • Disorganized thinking or writing

Behavioral Warning Signs

  • Noticeable changes in appearance or hygiene
  • Social withdrawal or isolation
  • Increased irritability or emotional outbursts
  • Unusual or concerning content in assignments
  • Excessive fatigue or appearing sleep-deprived

Emotional Warning Signs

  • Expressions of hopelessness or worthlessness
  • Excessive worry or fear
  • Flat affect or lack of emotional expression
  • Overwhelming stress or inability to cope
  • Expressions of self-harm or suicidal thoughts

Physical Warning Signs

  • Significant weight loss or gain
  • Frequent complaints of physical ailments
  • Visible signs of self-harm
  • Appearing disoriented or confused
  • Trembling or physical agitation

Important Note

The presence of these signs does not necessarily indicate a mental health condition, but they may warrant attention and support. When in doubt, consult with campus mental health professionals.

Intervention Approaches

The CARE Approach

C Connect

Establish rapport and a safe space for conversation. Choose a private location and express genuine concern.

A Acknowledge

Validate their feelings and experiences. Use specific observations rather than interpretations or judgments.

R Refer

Share information about appropriate campus resources and offer to help them connect with these services.

E Extend

Follow up appropriately while respecting boundaries and privacy. Maintain supportive communication.

Do's and Don'ts

Do's

  • Listen actively without judgment
  • Express concern using "I" statements
  • Respect privacy and confidentiality
  • Know your limits and campus resources
  • Document interactions appropriately

Don'ts

  • Promise confidentiality you can't maintain
  • Try to diagnose or provide therapy
  • Minimize their concerns or experiences
  • Take responsibility for their decisions
  • Wait if you see warning signs of crisis

Conversation Starters

Expressing Concern

  • "I've noticed [specific observation], and I'm concerned about you. How are you doing?"
  • "I wanted to check in with you because you seem [different/distracted/upset] lately."

Active Listening

  • "It sounds like you're feeling [reflect what you're hearing]. Is that right?"
  • "Can you tell me more about what's been going on?"

Offering Support

  • "There are resources on campus that might be helpful. Would you like me to tell you about them?"
  • "How can I best support you right now?"

Making Referrals

  • "The counseling center has helped many students with similar concerns. Would you like me to help you connect with them?"
  • "Would it be helpful if we walked over to the wellness center together?"

Referral Process Steps

  1. Identify the Need: Recognize signs that a student may benefit from professional support.
  2. Discuss Options: Talk to the student about available resources and why you think a referral is appropriate.
  3. Make the Referral: Provide specific information about the resource, including how to access it and what to expect.
  4. Follow Up: Check in with the student after the referral to see how they are doing and if they need further assistance.

Referral Resources

  • Campus Counseling Center: Offers free, confidential counseling services for students.
  • Wellness Center: Provides health services and wellness programs.
  • Academic Support Services: Helps with academic stress and time management.
  • Peer Support Groups: Connects students with peers facing similar challenges.
